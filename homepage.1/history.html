Jackson Heights: The 1900s To Now

Prior to what we know nowadays as Jackson Height, the community infused in culture from all the world, it was nonexistent during the 1900s. Before this time, Jackson Height was known as Trains Meadow section of Newtown (now Elmhurst) and consisted of farms. In 1901, the construction of the Queensboro Bridge, spanning from Manhattan to Long Island City, resulted in the development of streets, sewer systems and sidewalks from 70th to 92nd street. "Jackson" comes from John Jackson, the president of Hunters Point and Flushing Turnpike Company while "Heights" was used to make the community more appealing. Slowly, apartments grew throughout the community, beginning with rent ranging from $24 to $45. In 1917, a train station was built in Jackson Heights and as president of Queensboro Corporation, MacDougall sought to increase revenue by building apartments inspired by the "Garden City" movement. This movement was in reaction to the unsanitary life at tenements thus the new apartments in Jackson Heights were popular. However, as they continued to build these apartments, Anglo-Saxon Protestants moved in. By the 40s and 50s, Jewish and Latinx people arrived to New York, eventually Asian immigrants arrived with the removal of the 1965 Immigrant Act.

Fun Facts About Jackson Heights:

  • Nowadays Ittadi Garden & Grill was a movie theater for the newcomers which then became The Eagle, a Bollywood theater with a growing Indian community.
  • Immigrants make up 60% of the population of Jackson Heights.
  • 81% of residents speak a language other than English at home.
